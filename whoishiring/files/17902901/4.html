At GitLab we pay market rates instead of paying the same wage for the same role in different regions. Paying the same wage in different regions would lead to:

1. A concentration of team members in low-wage regions, since it is a better deal for them, while we want a geographically diverse team.

3. Team members in low-wage regions being in golden handcuffs and unlikely to leave even when they are unhappy.

4. If we start paying everyone the highest wage our compensation costs would increase greatly, we can hire fewer people, and we would get less results.

I appreciate the thought you've put into this. I've read the full linked page as well. I offer the following comments not because I think it will change your mind but because it appears that you want to be both careful and consistent in this process.

- The following two quotes appear contradictory.

"we want a geographically diverse team."

Contradiction: Stated motivation vs. declared decision criteria.

- The "Paying Local Rates" list could be re-organized as follows (programmer nitpick)

1. A concentration of team members in low-wage regions, since it is a better deal for them, while we want a geographically diverse team.

2. If we start paying everyone the highest wage our compensation costs would increase greatly, we can hire fewer people, and we would get less results.

3.1 Team members in high-wage regions [would have] less discretionary income then ones in low-wage countries with the same role.

- The "Relocating" section has weak rhetorical grounding.

Graph 3 - Status Quo Comparison - You'd have to quit for a non-remote friendly company.

Graph 4 - Status Quo Comparison - The best you could hope for is a pay cut.

These two paragraphs do not advance your values. A reader who needs persuading will see this as aiming for a low bar. Consider an alternative which poses this as a principled opposition to a broken system:

"We compensate based on normalized standard of living. To obtain a life with standard qualities 1., 2. and 3. we pay so that you can have those things. We are willing to pay the tax of high-rents in some areas to attract talent from all areas but offer a bonus relocation payment when engineers transfer to areas that lower our costs as well. We feel that the status quo is broken, some areas are over-priced, and that ultimately reward should be commensurate with the value the company derives from its employees."

Graph 5 - Adjusting compensation is fair.

A reader who disagrees with your policy will not agree that consistent == fair. The basic counter argument is that employee output should be a black box like any product, it you derive value X and are willing to pay value Y (assuming a socially acceptable profit margin), then as the consumer you should pay Y. The origin of the product should be a non-factor. Market purists feel it is a pretty fundamental violation akin to a claw-back to tell a producer they must immediately lower their rates when they implement efficiency gains in their production process. Imagine a gitlab customer coming to you and saying "Hey I heard you were able to reduce your incremental server costs by 10%! Well done! Where's my discount?"

Again you don't have to agree with this view but as your document is otherwise comprehensive you might want to address it.
